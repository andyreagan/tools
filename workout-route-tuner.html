How It Works

The problem: Structured cycling workouts (intervals, sweet spot, VO₂max, etc.) are designed for trainers where you control the environment. On the road, you hit stop signs, traffic lights, steep descents where you can't hold power, and hills where your gearing limits cadence. Services like BestBikeSplit optimize pacing for race performance, but training is different — you need physiologically appropriate intervals that fit your actual roads.

What this tool does: It takes your workout and your route, then simulates the ride using a physics model (aerodynamic drag, rolling resistance, gravity, rider weight). This tells you where on the road each interval lands. You mark stops and hazards, and the tool flags conflicts — like a 5-minute threshold interval that would have you blasting through a stop sign at km 3.2. You can then manually shorten intervals, add rest breaks for stops, or use the auto-suggest feature.

Physics model: Speed = f(power, grade, CdA, Crr, mass). At each point along the route, the tool computes the speed you'd sustain at the interval's target power on that grade. Integration over time gives distance covered per interval. This is the same model used by cycling physics simulators — Coggan's power-speed relationship.

Gearing check: For each segment, the tool calculates what gear ratio you'd need to maintain a cadence between your min and max at the resulting speed. On steep climbs at low speed, you may run out of gears. On fast descents, you may spin out.

Hill repeats: Mark turnaround points on the route to designate climb sections. Design your workout around the climb — hard efforts going up, recovery going down. The turnaround markers help you see exactly where to turn.

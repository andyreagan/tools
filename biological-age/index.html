About PhenoAge

PhenoAge was developed by Morgan Levine and colleagues at Yale, published in Aging (2018). It uses 9 blood biomarkers plus chronological age to estimate a "phenotypic age" that predicts all-cause mortality better than chronological age alone. A one-year increase in PhenoAge (relative to chronological age) is associated with a 9% increase in all-cause mortality risk.

The 9 biomarkers are available on a standard CBC (complete blood count) and CMP (comprehensive metabolic panel), with the exception of C-reactive protein (CRP), which must be ordered separately. All inputs are from a routine blood draw.

The model was trained on NHANES III (1988–1994) and validated on NHANES IV (1999–2018) with up to 12 years of mortality follow-up.

Citation: Levine ME, Lu AT, Quach A, et al. "An epigenetic biomarker of aging for lifespan and healthspan." Aging 2018;10(4):573–591. DOI: 10.18632/aging.101414

About the Aging.AI models

Aging.AI was a free online tool by Insilico Medicine that predicted chronological age from blood tests using deep neural networks. Three versions were released:

  • v1.0 (2016): Ensemble of 21 DNNs with ElasticNet meta-learner. 41 blood markers. Best single DNN: 4 hidden layers (2000→1500→1000→500), PReLU activation, AdaGrad optimizer. r=0.91, MAE=5.55 years. Trained on 62,419 Russian clinical samples.
  • v2.0: 33 markers, r=0.79, MAE=6.2 years. Never published separately.
  • v3.0 (2018): Single DNN, Leaky ReLU, EVE optimizer. 19 blood markers + sex + population. r=0.80, MAE=5.94 years. Trained on 142,379 samples from South Korea, Canada, and Eastern Europe. Validated on NHANES (R²=0.50).

The site (aging.ai) went offline circa 2024. The model weights were never published — only the architecture descriptions appeared in the papers. Archived versions of the site are available on the Wayback Machine.

v1.0: Putin E et al. "Deep biomarkers of human aging." Aging 2016;8(5):1021–1033. DOI: 10.18632/aging.100968
v3.0: Mamoshina P et al. "Population specific biomarkers of human aging." J Gerontol A 2018;73(11):1482–1490. DOI: 10.1093/gerona/gly005

About LifeScore Med360

Developed at MassMutual using 20 years of life insurance application data (~1.5 million records, 23,000 observed deaths). The random survival forest model used ~60 inputs from lab tests and health questionnaires to produce a 0–100 life score representing mortality-risk percentile within age/sex/smoking cohorts. It yielded a 9% reduction in claims within the healthiest risk pool compared to traditional underwriting.

A consumer-facing tool (MyLifeScore) was publicly available but the API (api.lifescoremodels.com) has been discontinued.

Maier M et al. "Improving the Accuracy and Transparency of Underwriting with Artificial Intelligence." AI Magazine 2020;41(3):78–93.

About KDM Biological Age

The Klemera-Doubal method (2006) takes a different approach: it fits linear regressions of each biomarker against age, then combines them into a biological age estimate weighted by each marker's correlation with age. Unlike PhenoAge's fixed coefficients, KDM requires training on a reference population, making it harder to implement as a standalone calculator. It is available in the BioAge R package (160+ stars) alongside PhenoAge and homeostatic dysregulation measures.

Klemera P, Doubal S. "A new approach to the concept and computation of biological age." Mech Ageing Dev 2006;127(3):240–248.
Kwon D, Belsky DW. "A toolkit for quantification of biological age from blood chemistry and organ function test data: BioAge." GeroScience 2021;43:2795–2808. DOI: 10.1007/s11357-021-00480-5

About physical activity & mortality

Beyond blood biomarkers, physical activity is a strong predictor of mortality. Fishman et al. (2016) used NHANES accelerometer data (2003–2006, n=3,029 adults aged 50–79) to show that those in the highest tertile of total activity had one-fifth the mortality risk of those in the lowest (HR: 0.21). Replacing just 30 minutes of daily sedentary time with light activity was associated with a 20% reduction in mortality risk at 5 years (HR: 0.80).

Fishman EI et al. "Association between Objectively Measured Physical Activity and Mortality in NHANES." Med Sci Sports Exerc 2016;48(7):1303–1311. DOI: 10.1249/MSS.0000000000000885
